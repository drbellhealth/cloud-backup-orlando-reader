Data Loss & Unplanned Downtime

Data Loss & Unplanned Downtime Unplanned downtime is expensive in ways that are easy to underestimate until it happens. The obvious cost is idle staff — people who cannot access files, run software, or serve customers. The less obvious costs include missed billing cycles, delayed project deliverables, and the hours spent on manual recovery attempts that may or may not succeed. For a law firm missing a court deadline or a dental practice unable to access patient records, there are consequences beyond lost revenue. The 3-2-1 backup rule — three copies of your data, on two different types of media, with one copy offsite — is a starting point for reducing downtime risk. It does not guarantee anything on its own, but it closes the most common single-point-of-failure gaps.

Ransomware & Backup-Targeted Attacks

Ransomware & Backup-Targeted Attacks The reason backup-targeted ransomware is now so common is straightforward: it works. If an attacker can reach and encrypt or delete your backup repository before you notice the intrusion, your options shrink to paying the ransom or rebuilding from scratch. The defense requires backup infrastructure that is architecturally separated from the rest of your network. Immutable storage (where data is locked against modification for a set period) and air-gapped copies (where there is no live network path to the backup) are the two most commonly recommended layers. It is also worth knowing that the average time between initial compromise and ransomware deployment has been measured in days or weeks — meaning attackers often have time to locate and neutralize standard network-attached backups before you know anything is wrong.

Compliance & Data-Retention Requirements (HIPAA, PCI, FTC Safeguards)

Compliance & Data-Retention Requirements (HIPAA, PCI, FTC Safeguards) For healthcare practices, HIPAA requires that covered entities protect electronic protected health information (ePHI) and maintain it in a way that can be recovered after an emergency. That includes a written contingency plan and documented backup procedures. The FTC Safeguards Rule, updated in recent years, imposes data security requirements on financial services businesses — including many accounting firms and insurance agencies — that include secure backup and access controls. PCI DSS covers businesses that process card payments. Each of these frameworks has specific retention periods and security requirements that a generic consumer backup product is unlikely to satisfy out of the box. Retention policies, encryption standards, and audit trail capabilities all need to be verified against the applicable rule, not assumed.

Failed, Untested & Silent Backups

Failed, Untested & Silent Backups One of the more common discoveries during a backup assessment is that backup jobs have been failing silently for an extended period — sometimes months. The job appears to run, no obvious error is surfaced, but the data being written is incomplete or corrupt. This happens for a range of reasons: a changed credential, a network path that moved, a storage limit that was quietly reached. The result is that an organization believes it has a backup when it does not. The only reliable guard against this is a monitoring process that verifies job completion and data integrity, combined with a scheduled restore test on actual recovery hardware. Checking that a backup completed is not the same as checking that it can be restored.

Hurricane-Season Disaster Recovery & Business Continuity

Hurricane-Season Disaster Recovery & Business Continuity Central Florida businesses have direct experience with what a major storm can do to physical infrastructure. Hurricane Ian in 2022 caused extensive damage across the region and left some businesses without access to their offices for days or longer. A business continuity plan for hurricane season needs to address not just data backup but the ability to operate from an alternate location using cloud-hosted systems. That means servers need to be recoverable in a cloud environment, not just restorable to physical hardware that may no longer be available. For businesses in flood-prone areas or those reliant on a single office location, testing the scenario — actually spinning up operations remotely — before a storm season is the only way to know the plan will hold.

When to Escalate Beyond Standard Backup Scope

When to Escalate Beyond Standard Backup Scope Standard managed backup covers most small-business scenarios adequately. But a few situations call for a more deliberate conversation with your provider. If your business is subject to multiple overlapping compliance frameworks — a dental practice that also processes card payments, for instance — the retention and security requirements need to be reviewed together, not in isolation. If your RTO requirement is measured in minutes rather than hours, standard backup scheduling may not be sufficient, and a more robust DRaaS arrangement with near-continuous replication is worth evaluating. If your business has recently experienced a security incident — even one that did not appear to affect backup — it is worth a full audit of backup infrastructure before assuming it is still clean. These are conversations worth having before a crisis, not during one.
