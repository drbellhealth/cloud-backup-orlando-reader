Orlando Cloud Backup & Disaster Recovery FAQ

Common questions Orlando-area businesses ask before choosing a cloud backup and disaster recovery provider — answered plainly.

What does cloud backup mean for a small business that is not technical?

At its simplest, cloud backup means copies of your business data — files, emails, databases, server contents — are automatically sent to secure storage outside your office, usually in a professional data center. If something goes wrong locally, whether that is a hardware failure, an accidental deletion, or a security incident, you can retrieve those copies and resume work. The cloud part just means the storage is managed infrastructure over the internet, rather than a tape or external drive in your office. For a non-technical owner, the most important thing to understand is that the backup needs to be monitored regularly and test-restored periodically to confirm it actually works.

Does Microsoft 365 back up my emails and files on its own?

No — not in the way most business owners assume. Microsoft operates under what is called a shared-responsibility model. They keep their own infrastructure running and available, but protecting your specific data from accidental deletion, a disgruntled employee, or a ransomware attack is your responsibility, not theirs. Microsoft 365's own retention and recycle-bin features have time limits and do not cover all scenarios. A separate Microsoft 365 backup solution — one that pulls copies of your mailboxes, SharePoint, OneDrive, and Teams data into storage you control — is the correct approach. What do RTO and RPO mean and why do they matter?

RTO stands for Recovery Time Objective — it is the maximum amount of time you can afford to be offline after a failure before the business impact becomes unacceptable. RPO stands for Recovery Point Objective — it is the maximum amount of data loss you can tolerate, measured in time. If your RPO is four hours, it means losing up to four hours of work is survivable; if it is fifteen minutes, you need much more frequent backup snapshots. These two numbers should drive your backup design. A provider who does not ask about your RTO and RPO in the first conversation is probably selling you a product rather than solving a problem.

What is the 3-2-1 backup rule?

The 3-2-1 rule is a widely used guideline: keep three copies of your data, stored on at least two different types of media, with one copy located offsite. In practice, that might mean your working data on a server, a local backup on a NAS device at your office, and a cloud copy at a geographically separate data center. The point is to eliminate single points of failure. If your building floods, the offsite copy survives. If your cloud provider has an outage, the local copy is available. No single event should be able to take out all three copies.

What is an immutable backup and does my business need one?

An immutable backup is a copy of your data stored in a way that cannot be modified or deleted — not even by an administrator account — for a defined period. This is specifically designed to survive ransomware attacks that have obtained elevated network credentials and are attempting to destroy your recovery options before you notice the breach. Whether your business needs immutable storage depends on your risk profile and the sensitivity of your data. For healthcare practices, legal firms, and financial services businesses in the Orlando area, the answer is generally yes. How often should a backup be tested?

At minimum, a restore test should happen quarterly. For critical systems — a server running your billing software or practice management application — monthly testing is more appropriate. The test needs to go beyond confirming that the backup job completed; it should involve actually restoring data to a test environment and verifying it is intact and functional. A backup that has only ever been written, never restored, is not a tested backup. Ask any provider you are considering how often they perform documented restore tests and what the process looks like.

What should Central Florida businesses know about hurricane-season backup planning?

The key consideration for hurricane preparedness is not just whether your data is backed up, but whether your business can operate if the physical office is inaccessible for several days. That requires a cloud-based recovery option — the ability to spin up your servers and applications in a hosted environment rather than restoring to hardware in a building you cannot enter. Hurricane Ian in 2022 illustrated how quickly physical access assumptions can fail. A business continuity plan for hurricane season should include a specific test of remote operations before each storm season begins, not a theoretical plan that has never been executed. How does ransomware attack backup systems and what can stop it?

Modern ransomware operators look for connected backup repositories early in an intrusion, because eliminating your recovery option is the most effective way to ensure payment. Standard network-attached backups — drives connected to your server or a cloud sync folder with live credentials — are reachable by ransomware that has obtained those credentials. The technical defenses are immutable storage (data locked against modification for a set period) and air-gapped copies (no live network connection between your environment and the backup location). Neither is complicated to implement with the right provider, but neither happens automatically with a basic backup product.

What is the difference between backup and disaster recovery?

Backup is the process of making copies of your data. Disaster recovery is the broader plan for how you get your business operational again after a significant failure — which usually involves those backups, but also requires tested processes, defined time targets, and in some cases pre-provisioned cloud infrastructure to run on. You can have good backups and still have a poor disaster recovery outcome if the restore process has never been practiced, the replacement hardware is not available, or nobody knows which systems to bring up first. Disaster Recovery as a Service (DRaaS) bundles the infrastructure and the process together under a managed arrangement with a defined RTO.

Do compliance requirements like HIPAA affect how I have to handle backups?

Yes, for most regulated industries they do. HIPAA requires healthcare covered entities to have a data backup plan as part of a written contingency plan, and that plan must include procedures to restore lost data. The FTC Safeguards Rule places similar obligations on financial services firms, including many accounting and insurance businesses. Retention periods, encryption standards, and access controls all have to be aligned to the applicable rule. A generic consumer backup product or an unconfigured cloud sync is unlikely to satisfy these requirements without additional configuration. If your business is in a regulated vertical, this is worth raising explicitly in any provider conversation.
